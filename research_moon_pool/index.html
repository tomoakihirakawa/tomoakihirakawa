    In an earlier paper (Molin 2001), a theoretical model, based on linearized potential flow theory, was proposed to derive the natural frequencies and associated modal shapes of the piston and sloshing modes in rectangular moonpools. This model was based on simplifying assumptions: the floating support is motionless, the water depth is infinite, and the beam and length of the support are taken to the limit where they are of infinite extent. The geometry is then equivalent to a rectangular opening in a rigid ice-sheet. The fluid domain is divided into two sub-domains: the moonpool and a semi-infinite domain below the keel line. Eigenfunction expansions are used to describe the velocity potential inside the moonpool. The connection with the flow in the lower fluid domain is ensured via an integral equation relating the velocity potential to its vertical derivative, at the base of the moonpool. Then an eigenvalue problem is formulated and solved numerically.ã€€

    2.3. Numerical results and validations
    Here we present some comparisons between natural frequencies obtained with our theoretical models and natural frequencies calculated with WAMIT. We take the moonpool radius a as 10 m and its draft d as 5 m and we vary the clearance c and the outer radius b. Figure 3 shows one of the panellings used in WAMIT for b = 2 a = 20 m. In the WAMIT computations the floater is successively undergoing forced heave motion (to track the axisymmetric modes) then forced surge motion (for the antisymmetric modes). Response amplitude operators (RAOs) of the free surface elevation are obtained at selected locations in the moonpool. From peaks of the RAOs plotted versus the wave frequency, the resonant frequencies of the considered modes are derived. The frequency step is progressively refined around the peaks to increase accuracy.
